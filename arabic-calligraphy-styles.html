The Two Foundation Scripts

All Arabic calligraphy styles evolved from two foundational scripts: Kufic and Naskh. Understanding these two is key to appreciating the entire tradition.

Kufic represents the angular, geometric approach to Arabic writing, while Naskh represents the cursive, flowing approach. Over centuries, calligraphers developed numerous variations and entirely new styles based on these foundations, each serving different purposes ‚Äî from religious manuscripts to royal decrees to everyday correspondence.

üí° Did You Know?

The earliest copies of the Quran were written in Kufic script. Today, both Kufic and Naskh-derived styles are used for Quranic text, with Naskh being more common in printed editions due to its superior readability.

Kufic Script

7th Century ‚Ä¢ Kufa, Iraq

Kufic is one of the oldest and most iconic Arabic calligraphy styles. It originated in the city of Kufa in Iraq during the 7th century and was the primary script used to write the earliest copies of the Quran.

The script is instantly recognizable by its angular, geometric shapes with straight horizontal and vertical lines meeting at sharp angles. This bold, architectural quality made Kufic perfect for monumental inscriptions on mosques, palaces, and coins throughout the Islamic world.

Over time, numerous Kufic variations emerged including floriated Kufic (with floral decorations), square Kufic (purely geometric), and interlaced Kufic (with interweaving letters). Today, Kufic remains popular for logos, architectural decorations, and modern design applications due to its striking geometric aesthetic.

Naskh Script

10th Century ‚Ä¢ Baghdad

Naskh, meaning "to copy" in Arabic, is the most widely used Arabic calligraphy style today. It's the script you see in most printed books, newspapers, and digital Arabic text. Its rounded, flowing form makes it highly readable ‚Äî essential for transcribing lengthy texts.

The script was popularized by the legendary calligrapher Ibn Muqla al-Shirazi in the 9th century. He systematized Arabic calligraphy by establishing proportional rules based on the rhombic dot, ensuring consistency and beauty across all letters.

Naskh's balanced, clean appearance makes it ideal for both religious texts and secular publications. If you're looking for a style that's elegant yet easy to read, Naskh is an excellent choice ‚Äî especially for names that need to be clearly legible.

Thuluth Script

7th Century ‚Ä¢ Islamic Golden Age

Thuluth is considered the most beautiful and prestigious of all Arabic calligraphy styles. Its name means "one-third" ‚Äî referring to the proportion of curved to straight lines in each letter. Master calligraphers consider Thuluth the ultimate test of skill; traditionally, a calligrapher wasn't recognized as a master until they had perfected this style.

The script features dramatic elongated letters, sweeping curves, and elegant proportions that create visually stunning compositions. Unlike Naskh, Thuluth was rarely used for writing Qurans due to its decorative nature. Instead, it adorned mosque walls, book titles, and ceremonial documents.

Perhaps the most famous example of Thuluth calligraphy is the kiswah ‚Äî the black cloth covering the Kaaba in Mecca, embroidered with golden Quranic verses in Thuluth script. For Arabic calligraphy tattoos and personalized artwork, Thuluth remains the most popular choice due to its undeniable elegance.

Diwani Script

16th Century ‚Ä¢ Ottoman Empire

Diwani script emerged in the Ottoman Empire during the 16th century and quickly became the official script of the royal court ‚Äî "diwan" meaning royal court in Turkish. Its ornate, flowing style served both aesthetic and practical purposes: the intricate letterforms were difficult to forge, making Diwani ideal for official documents and decrees.

The style is characterized by rounded letters that often interweave and connect in complex patterns. Letters may sit above, below, or alongside each other, creating dense, decorative compositions. A more elaborate version called Diwani Jali ("polished Diwani") adds even more decorative elements and fills the spaces between letters with ornamental dots.

Today, Diwani is beloved for wedding invitations, certificates, and luxury designs. Its romantic, flowing quality makes it perfect for names and phrases meant to convey elegance and sophistication.

Ruqaa Script

Ottoman Era ‚Ä¢ Practical Writing

Ruqaa is the everyday handwriting style of the Arab world. Developed for practical, fast writing, it strips away the elaborate flourishes of other styles in favor of simplicity and speed. If you've seen handwritten Arabic notes, signs, or casual correspondence, you've likely seen Ruqaa.

The script features short, simplified strokes with minimal decorative elements. Letters connect smoothly with small movements, allowing writers to maintain a quick pace. Despite its simplicity, skilled calligraphers can elevate Ruqaa into beautiful, clean compositions.

Ruqaa works well for casual designs, signatures, and projects where you want an authentic "handwritten" Arabic feel without the formality of scripts like Thuluth or Diwani.

Other Important Arabic Calligraphy Styles

Beyond the five major styles above, several other scripts deserve mention for their historical importance and unique aesthetics:

Developed in Persia (Iran) in the 14th-15th centuries, Nastaliq is the dominant script for Urdu, Persian, and Pashto languages. It features a distinctive downward slope from right to left, creating a flowing, poetic appearance. Nastaliq is particularly associated with Persian poetry and remains essential for South Asian Islamic calligraphy.

Meaning "accomplished" or "clear," Muhaqqaq was once considered among the most beautiful scripts for Quranic text. It features tall, elongated letters with sweeping horizontal strokes. Though rarely used today, historical Muhaqqaq manuscripts are prized museum pieces representing the peak of medieval Islamic calligraphy.

Named after the sweet-smelling rayhan plant (basil), Rayhani is characterized by its readability and aesthetic balance. How to Choose the Right Arabic Calligraphy Style

Selecting the right style depends on your project's purpose, audience, and aesthetic goals. Here's a practical guide:

For Names and Personal Art

Thuluth is the classic choice for names due to its elegance and visual impact. If you want something more ornate and romantic, try Diwani. For a modern, geometric look, Kufic offers striking results.

For Tattoos

Thuluth works best for tattoos ‚Äî its bold, clear lines translate well to skin and remain legible at various sizes. Avoid overly intricate styles like Diwani Jali, which may blur over time.

For Wedding Invitations

Diwani is the traditional choice for wedding stationery in the Arab world. Its romantic, flowing quality perfectly suits the occasion. Thuluth also works well for more formal, classical invitations.

For Logos and Branding

Kufic is ideal for modern logos due to its geometric, scalable nature. Square Kufic in particular works beautifully in contemporary brand identities, as the angular forms adapt well to digital applications.

For Readable Text

Naskh is your best choice when readability is the priority. It's the standard for books, documents, and any text meant to be read at length.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the main types of Arabic calligraphy?

The main types of Arabic calligraphy are Kufic, Naskh, Thuluth, Diwani, Ruqaa, Nastaliq, and Muhaqqaq. Kufic and Naskh are the two foundational scripts from which most other styles evolved. Each style has unique characteristics developed over centuries for different purposes.

What is the difference between Naskh and Thuluth calligraphy?

Naskh is a simpler, more readable script with rounded, balanced letters ‚Äî commonly used for books and everyday text. Thuluth is more decorative and prestigious, featuring dramatic elongated curves and elegant proportions. Naskh prioritizes readability; Thuluth prioritizes visual beauty.

Which Arabic calligraphy style is best for names?

Thuluth and Diwani are the most popular choices for names due to their elegant, decorative appearance. For tattoos, Thuluth is recommended for its bold, clear lines. For wedding invitations, Diwani adds a romantic touch. For modern logos, Kufic provides a geometric, contemporary aesthetic.

What is the oldest Arabic calligraphy style?

Kufic is one of the oldest Arabic calligraphy styles, originating in the 7th century in Kufa, Iraq. It was used to write the earliest copies of the Quran and was the dominant script for Islamic inscriptions for several centuries before cursive styles like Naskh gained popularity.
